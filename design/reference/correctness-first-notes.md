# Does correctness-first make the faithfulness capstone land harder — or tacked-on?

## It lands harder. Decisively.

Putting faithfulness last is the single biggest improvement to how the lesson *feels*, for one reason: **the capstone now has a foil.** When faithfulness opened the lesson, "the judge can be wrong" was an abstract warning with nothing to compare it to — the newcomer had no prior model of what a *trustworthy* evaluator looked like, so a fallible one wasn't shocking, just confusing.

Correctness-first builds the contrast the capstone needs:

1. **Beat 1 (diff)** gives them a perfect evaluator — free, instant, identical every time. They learn what *certainty* feels like.
2. **Beat 2 (reference judge)** cracks it: the answer is fuzzy, the diff is useless, and they have to trust an LLM to compare meaning — but **they still hold an expected answer as an anchor.** One safety rail remains.
3. **Beat 3 (faithfulness)** removes the last rail: *there is no expected answer.* The judge is now the only thing standing between them and a hallucination — and they watch it wave one through because of a single word in its instructions.

The escalation is **diff (certain) → reference judge (fuzzy, but anchored) → faithfulness (fuzzy and unanchored)**. By the time "the evaluator is fallible" arrives, the user has *felt* two more reliable evaluators, so the fallible one isn't an opener's shrug — it's a genuine "oh no" moment. The stakes are highest exactly where you can least check it, and they arrive at that sentence having earned it.

## The one risk, and the mitigation

The danger of "last" is feeling **tacked-on** — a fourth wall after the lesson seemed done. Two things prevent that:

- **Motivate it by the previous beat's limit, never by chronology.** Beat 3 opens on a question the user is now equipped to ask — *"but what if there's no answer to write down at all?"* — not "and now, lesson 3." Each beat's CTA is the next beat's reason to exist. (Built this way: the buttons read "But what about answers you can't string-match?" → "But what if there's no answer to write down at all?")
- **Make it the hardest, not the longest.** Faithfulness should feel like the summit, not an appendix. Framing it as "the soul of evals — the evaluator is itself fallible, and the stakes are highest where you can least check it" gives it gravity the earlier beats deliberately lack.

## Net

Faithfulness was the original app's centerpiece because it's the *technically* deepest idea. Correctness-first keeps it as the *emotionally* deepest moment while fixing the pedagogy: the newcomer now arrives at it fluent in the vocabulary (evaluator, golden set, reference) and carrying two cleaner mental models to contrast against. It's no longer the confusing door you walk in through — it's the room at the top of the stairs.
